the storyBiography

Todd English started cooking at fifteen. He graduated with honors from the Culinary Institute of America, trained under Jean-Jacques Rachou at La Côte Basque, then left for Italy and came back with an idea that changed American dining: rustic Mediterranean food, cooked with fire and conviction. Olives opened in Charlestown in 1989 with fifty seats and a wood oven; within a decade it was a global name.

Five James Beard Awards followed. So did television, fifteen cookbooks' worth of ideas, restaurants on four continents, and a generation of cooks who came up through his kitchens. Bon Appétit named him Restaurateur of the Year; People put him on the 50 Most Beautiful list — the first chef who was equal parts craftsman and celebrity.

Today, English runs the plays he invented through The English Hospitality Group, founded in 2022 with partner Keith Burkard — with active restaurants from Walt Disney World to Bali, a 24/7 food hall in Abu Dhabi, and a residents-only concept rising sixty-two stories above Miami.

“I cook the way I live — loud, generous, and a little bit reckless.”

Todd English

The House That Olives Built

In 1989, rustic Mediterranean food was not a category in American dining — it was a bet. Todd and Olivia English opened Olives in a Charlestown storefront with fifty seats, a wood-burning oven, and a no-reservations policy that turned the sidewalk into a nightly event. The butternut squash tortelli became a Boston legend; the room became a school.

Olives grew the way few restaurants ever have — to the Bellagio in Las Vegas, to New York, Washington, Aspen and Tokyo — and its alumni went on to run kitchens across the country. When the last location closed in 2023, the concept had outlived nearly every restaurant trend it predated.

The name is not retired. Olives Black — a three-floor evolution announced for 15 Park Row in New York — is designed to bring the flagship home. The oven, as ever, comes first.
