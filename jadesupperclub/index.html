Part I I

The House

✦

A green velvet corridor, a lit slab of onyx, and a room that does not tell you what time it is.

the storyJade Supper Club

Manila has always known how to stay up late. What it did not have was one room that could hold the whole night — dinner worth dressing for, a band worth staying for, and a bar that is still pouring when the kitchen has gone quiet. Jade was built to be that room.

It begins as a corridor. Green velvet on both sides, brass underfoot, and at the end a single slab of backlit jade onyx that glows like a held breath. You do not see the dining room until you are inside it — and then it opens: a double-height space in deep emerald and warm brass, banquettes running the long wall, a proscenium stage at the far end, and a spiral staircase in polished brass climbing to a mezzanine that looks down on all of it.

The cooking is fire-led and Manila-anchored: a wood oven at the centre of the kitchen, an ice bar for the raw list, Philippine seafood and Japanese precision meeting Mediterranean technique. The menu was developed in partnership with chef Todd English, whose five James Beard Awards and thirty-five years of wood-fired cooking sit behind the pass — his first fine-dining room in the Philippines.

“A supper club is not a restaurant with a band. It is one evening, written in three acts, and the food is only the first.”

An Evening at Jade

  1. 6:00

    Doors

    The corridor lights come up. First seating in the Grand Room; the ice bar is dressed and the oven is at temperature.

  2. 6:30

    The Ice Bar

    Oysters, crudo and hand rolls off the raw list. A pianist plays the room quietly — no vocal yet.

  3. 8:00

    Peak Service

    The wood oven runs flat out. Whole lapu-lapu, dry-aged ribeye and the flatbread that started it all.

  4. 9:00

    First Set

    The proscenium curtain opens. Forty-five minutes — jazz-leaning, with a vocalist, mixed live for the whole floor.

  5. 10:00

    Second Seating

    The room turns. Later tables arrive dressed for the music rather than the menu; the bar takes over the front.

  6. 11:00

    Second Set

    Louder, later, closer to the floor. The mezzanine fills; the spiral staircase becomes the best seat in the house.

  7. 12:00

    The Kitchen Closes

    Last order from the full menu. Bar bites — the Jade Dragon, Midnight in Manila — run until the end.

  8. 1:00

    The Late Room

    Lights down two stops. Resident DJ, sake carafes, and the part of the night the regulars come for.

  9. 2:00

    Last Call

    The brass panels dim one at a time. The corridor is the last thing you see, exactly as it was the first.

Three Acts

Dinner, music, late. Every element of the house — lighting, menu, staffing, sound — is built to hand the night from one act to the next without a seam.

Booking the Night

No. 03 — The Pass · Open to the Grand Room

The Wood Oven

Everything at Jade is organised around one piece of equipment. The oven runs at 400°C from four in the afternoon until midnight and never fully cools — bread in the early evening, whole fish and dry-aged beef at peak, focaccia for the bar when the dining room has gone.

The technique is chef English's: fire, salt, time, and nothing hidden behind a sauce. The sourcing is Manila's — lapu-lapu from Palawan boats, Cebu oysters, Ilocos garlic, Batangas coffee in the dessert. Japanese precision sits on top of it: the ice bar, the sake list, the uni.

At a Glance

No. 04 — The Proscenium · Far End of the Grand Room

The Stage

A full proscenium with a working curtain, sightlines from every banquette, and a room tuned so that a vocalist can be heard at the back without a table having to stop talking. Two sets a night — 9PM and 11PM — jazz-leaning early, looser and later after midnight.

The programme is Filipino-first: resident trio, rotating vocalists, and a monthly guest night that pulls from Manila's session players. The stage is also the reason the room works for takeovers — it is built for a set, not for background music.

the craftMaking the Room

Four materials, used everywhere and mixed nowhere else. This is the whole palette.

Material · 01

Backlit Jade Onyx

The single slab at the end of the corridor, lit from behind so the veining reads. It reappears as the bar counter and the stair treads — nowhere else.

Material · 02

Glowing Brass

Ribbed brass panels, backlit at a fixed warmth, run the full height of the atrium. They are the room's only light source above eye level.

Material · 03

Emerald Velvet

Deep cool green — never warm, never yellow. Banquettes, drapery, the corridor walls and the stage curtain, all cut from one dye lot.

Material · 04

Polished Black Stone

The floor. It reflects the brass and doubles the height of the room — the reason photographs of Jade always look taller than the space is.

The Chef Partner

Todd English trained under Jean-Jacques Rachou at La Côte Basque, then left for Italy and came back with rustic Mediterranean food cooked over fire. Olives opened in Charlestown in 1989 with fifty seats and a wood oven. Five James Beard Awards followed, along with fifteen cookbooks and restaurants on four continents.

Jade is the first fine-dining room in the Philippines to carry his hand on the menu. The wood oven, the whole-fish service and the guéridon are his grammar, rewritten for Manila's produce. The kitchen is run day to day by its own resident brigade; Chef English sets the menu, returns for each seasonal change, and cooks the opening service himself.

Midnight to 2AM — The Bar, The Spiral & The Mezzanine

The Late Room

At midnight the kitchen closes the carte and the house changes shape. Lights drop two stops, the tables along the banquette run are cleared to standing, the resident DJ takes the stage, and the bar becomes the centre of gravity. The only food is bar bites — the Jade Dragon, Midnight in Manila, focaccia straight out of the cooling oven.

It is the part of the night the regulars book for, and the reason Jade is a supper club rather than a restaurant: nobody is asked to leave until two.

The Road to October

  1. Now

    The Shell, Signed

    Three months of advance rent and three months of security deposit — ₱3,373,966 — are placed with the landlord. Design is set with BA8 Concepts, Jerikson Construction is on site, and the mezzanine is with the structural engineer for sign-off.

  2. Aug

    Permits & Long Leads

    Permits and licences at ₱400,000 are lodged, and the point-of-sale goes in for BIR accreditation — a fixed three-to-four-week clock that gates opening day more tightly than the build does. Fire suppression, smoke detection and air conditioning are coordinated with building management.

  3. Sep

    Systems & the Kitchen

    Automated lighting, exhaust and ducting, CCTV and biometrics. The cold line, the hot line and the Josper charcoal oven are delivered and wired. Custom furniture installation begins, and the bar's ice cap goes in.

  4. Oct

    Staff, Stock, Rehearsal

    Kitchenware, bar and glassware at ₱1,100,000. Hiring closes on the forty-four-strong opening roster and one month of paid training begins at ₱1,135,960. Menu R&D runs against ₱6,000,000 of opening stock. Plants and soft finishes land last.

  5. Open
